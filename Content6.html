Processing Cocoa Beans

Opening the Pods

Do not wait more than 4 days to open the pods. Use a thick piece of wood to hit the pods open, then take the beans out and place them in baskets. Transport the baskets to the fermentation area. Broken husks should not be left in the field; they can be used to make compost.

Why Cocoa Beans Are Fermented

Cocoa beans are fermented to destroy the seed coat, kill the germ, and develop a good taste. The process improves the quality and flavor of the cocoa beans.

How to Ferment the Beans

Cocoa beans are often fermented in heaps. Choose a flat, dry spot, cover it with banana leaves, and pile the beans on top. Cover the heap with banana leaves and stir it from time to time for effective fermentation.

For better results, use fermentation boxes with holes at the bottom, placed on supports like stones. Juice will drain through the holes. After two days, transfer the beans to another box, stirring them well. Stack boxes for efficiency. Avoid keeping beans in the same box for more than two days. The fermentation process takes 6 to 10 days. Beans start purple and turn reddish when properly fermented.

Drying Cocoa Beans

Once fermented, cocoa beans need to be dried. Spread beans on boards elevated 1 meter above ground. The layer should be no thicker than 4 centimeters. Stir frequently and protect from rain. A sliding tray drier can be used to shelter the beans during wet conditions.

In moist forest regions, drying beans can be challenging. Consider using a modern drier with a concrete slab and a heat source. Ensure the fire is not too hot to prevent smoke and stir the beans frequently to avoid burning.

Sorting and Storing

After drying, sort the beans to remove flat, germinated, moldy, and broken beans. Keep only the good beans. Place them in sacks and store in a dry, animal-proof place. Sell the sacks of cocoa once they meet the quality standards: well-harvested, well-fermented, and well-dried.
